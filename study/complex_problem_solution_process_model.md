# Complex Problem Solution Process Model

## Introduction
The Complex Problem Solution Process Model is a structured approach for solving complex, ill-defined, and non-immediate but important problems. It is designed for situations in which the right answer is not obvious, where multiple causes may interact, and where jumping directly to a solution often leads to wasted effort or solving the wrong problem. The model organizes problem solving as a staged progression from understanding the problem to diagnosing its causes, generating and selecting solutions, implementing those solutions, and reviewing the process for continuous improvement.

At its core, the model follows five connected stages: `WHAT`, `WHY`, `HOW`, `DO`, and `REVIEW`. Each stage has a distinct purpose. `WHAT` frames the problem and defines the boundaries of the effort. `WHY` diagnoses root causes. `HOW` develops and selects alternative solutions. `DO` focuses on execution, communication, leadership, and project management. `REVIEW` ensures that the process remains adaptive, self-correcting, and suitable for future use.

The model assumes that high-quality problem solving depends on careful framing, evidence-based reasoning, disciplined hypothesis testing, explicit decision criteria, and effective stakeholder communication. It also assumes that complex problems require both breadth and depth of thinking. The ideal problem solver is therefore "T-shaped": broad enough to connect ideas across disciplines, and deep enough to reason rigorously within relevant domains.

## WHAT: Frame the Problem
Problem solving begins with framing. Framing is critical because the way a problem is defined strongly influences how it is understood and therefore how it will be solved. If the frame is flawed, the rest of the effort may be built on a weak foundation.

The first task is to identify the problem clearly. A problem definition card helps establish what the project is, what it is not, who the decision makers are, what resources are available, how much time is expected for each phase, and what risks must be managed. This card forces clarity and prevents teams from working with inconsistent assumptions.

Problem framing requires both divergent and convergent thinking. Divergent thinking helps generate multiple possible ways to describe the problem. Convergent thinking helps select the frame that is most useful. This stage also requires resisting conditioning. Past solutions may no longer fit present circumstances, and intuition alone is often unreliable when the stakes are high or the environment has changed.

Because complex problems are often ill-defined, the model emphasizes deliberate System 2 thinking over fast, intuitive judgment. Teams are encouraged to defer judgment, involve other people, keep probing, and rely on evidence rather than confidence. Members should continuously ask whether the current understanding reflects reality or whether it has been distorted by assumptions, habits, or overly narrow constraints.

Framing also includes defining scope. Teams must agree on project objectives, scope boundaries, and what success will look like. The scope should be neither too broad nor too narrow. A scope that is too broad makes the analysis unfocused; a scope that is too narrow may exclude the real source of the problem. New evidence discovered later may require changing the scope, and such changes should be reflected explicitly in the working definition of the problem.

An essential part of framing is forming the key diagnostic question. The model distinguishes between `what`, `why`, and `how` questions. In practice, the most important analytical questions are usually `why` and `how`. A `why` question is diagnostic: it seeks root causes. A `how` question is prescriptive: it seeks alternative solutions. The model warns against asking `how` before `why` has been understood. Good key questions are chosen for their type, topic, scope, and phrasing. Every word matters because imprecise language often signals weak logic.

To communicate the frame clearly, the model recommends an introductory flow based on `situation`, `complication`, and `question`. The situation defines the current reality using only necessary, sufficient, concise, and uncontested information. The complication introduces the disruption or tension that creates the need for change. The key question then becomes the logical destination of the situation-complication sequence. This structure helps audiences understand not just what the problem is, but why it matters now.

The outputs of the framing stage include a problem definition card, a properly scoped key question, an introductory flow, and a why card that summarizes the diagnostic frame without yet attempting to diagnose the problem itself.

## WHY: Diagnose the Problem
Once the problem is framed, the next stage is diagnosis. Diagnosis seeks to determine what is actually causing the problem. The model treats this as a structured, hypothesis-led, evidence-based process.

Diagnosis begins with root-cause identification. Teams identify the diagnostic key question and then use divergent and convergent thinking to surface and refine possible root causes. These causes are organized in diagnostic issue maps. The purpose of an issue map is to decompose a key question into manageable branches while preserving logic and completeness.

The model references many mapping tools, including fault trees, logic trees, decision trees, issue trees, value hierarchies, objective hierarchies, probability trees, Ishikawa diagrams, why-why diagrams, influence diagrams, evidence maps, argument maps, concept maps, dialogue maps, Bayesian networks, and other forms of structured analysis. The specific diagram matters less than the discipline it imposes: the analysis must move from the key question toward conclusions in a way that is explicit and reviewable.

A strong issue map answers a single type of question, makes the structure of the problem explicit, and lays out a manageable set of hypotheses. Diagnostic maps answer `why` questions; solution maps answer `how` questions. Each meaningful element of the map should be linked to a formal hypothesis that can be tested. The model encourages vertical moves to uncover deeper causal layers and horizontal moves to capture alternative explanations at the same level.

The structure of the map should be MECE: mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive. The answers themselves should be independent and collectively exhaustive. Teams are encouraged to generate possible answers before committing to a structure, defer criticism during generation, and continue drilling only while additional detail brings practical value. A map should be insightful, not merely exhaustive. Teams should ask the "so what?" question to judge whether a branch actually improves understanding.

The model warns against cognitive traps that often corrupt diagnosis, including fixation, premature closure, anchoring, overconfidence, and confirmation bias. These biases reduce the ability to search broadly, compare alternatives fairly, and update judgments when evidence changes.

After the map is built, teams develop and manage hypotheses. Each hypothesis should be testable, clearly phrased, related directly to the key question, and linked to a specific part of the map. Comparative hypotheses should be used where useful, and the number of active hypotheses should remain manageable. Broad hypotheses may cover unlikely parts of the map, while more concrete hypotheses address the more plausible explanations.

Testing hypotheses requires disciplined analysis. The model highlights Gauch's Presuppositions-Evidence-Logic framework. Every conclusion depends on presuppositions, evidence, and logic. Presuppositions define what is being assumed. Evidence provides the data. Logic connects assumptions and evidence to a conclusion. Full disclosure of these three elements is essential for transparent reasoning.

The model recommends using deduction, induction, and abduction together. Deduction applies a general rule to a specific case. Induction generalizes from observations. Abduction proposes the most plausible explanation for surprising observations. Strong diagnosis moves both forward from hypotheses to expected evidence and backward from evidence to candidate hypotheses.

Not all analysis is worthwhile. The model cautions against pseudodiagnosing, overdiagnosing, and misdiagnosing. Teams should ask what they are looking for, what they would do differently if they found it, and whether the information would change the course of action. If it would not, they should not spend time collecting it.

Evidence quality is therefore central. Evidence is often incomplete, inconclusive, ambiguous, dissonant, and imperfectly credible. It should be evaluated for relevance, credibility, and inferential force. Tangible evidence should be tested for authenticity, accuracy, and reliability. Testimonial evidence should be examined for basis of assertion, veracity, objectivity, and observational sensitivity.

The relationship between evidence and hypotheses must also be stated clearly. Evidence may support, favor, confirm, or validate a hypothesis. It may also oppose, undermine, rebut, refute, contradict, challenge, or disconfirm it. Strong diagnosis requires both supporting and opposing evidence. Teams are encouraged to facilitate serendipity, because some useful findings appear unexpectedly during investigation.

To decide among competing explanations, the model promotes Bayesian reasoning and Occam's Razor. Bayesian inference updates the probability of a hypothesis as new evidence is gathered. Occam's Razor suggests that, all else equal, the simplest explanation that accounts for the observations should be preferred. Diagnosis ends when the analysis is sufficiently conclusive to justify moving forward. At that point, the issue map captures the evidence, the thought process, and the synthesis behind the diagnostic conclusion.

## HOW: Find and Select Solutions
Only after diagnosis should the process move to solutions. This stage transforms understanding into action options. Its purpose is not to immediately choose one answer, but first to generate multiple plausible alternatives and then evaluate them using explicit criteria.

Solution development begins by forming a solution key question and constructing a solution issue map. Like diagnostic maps, solution maps should answer a single type of question, move from key question to conclusions, use MECE branches where possible, and remain insightful. The main difference is that solution maps seek alternatives rather than causes.

The model insists that teams look for alternatives, not sequential processes. A solution map should identify different ways to address the problem rather than merely list implementation steps. During idea generation, desirability and feasibility should be deferred temporarily. The goal is to expand the option set before narrowing it.

Divergent and convergent thinking are used again here. Teams are encouraged to brainstorm, brainwrite, use the Delphi method, work with analogies, challenge assumptions, think about opposites, dissect the problem, debate possibilities, evaluate creativity, and give the subconscious time to work. Creativity is improved when teams encourage unusual ideas, aim for quantity before quality, and use one idea to stimulate others.

The model also recognizes the limits of group ideation. Social loafing, conformity, production blocking, and downward norm setting can all reduce the effectiveness of brainstorming. Receptivity is therefore essential. Teams must create an environment where ideas are not dismissed prematurely and where diverse contributions are treated seriously.

As the map develops, each node should add value. Branches should be parallel and meaningful. Teams should identify what variable changes at each node and what states that variable can take. Branches do not all need the same depth, but they should remain useful, explicit, and logically consistent. Every significant element should be linked to a formal hypothesis or testable proposition.

Once a broad set of solution alternatives has been generated, the process moves to selection. The model recommends first removing alternatives that are clearly infeasible, undesirable, illegal, unethical, or otherwise inappropriate. Remaining options are then compared using evidence and formal decision tools.

The model references Gauch's full-disclosure principle, van Gelder's screen, and Leebron's SAILS framework. These encourage teams to examine whether a solution is strategically, financially, operationally, prudentially, ethically, and legally sound, and whether it is accountable, impactful, leveraged, and sustainable.

Evidence remains important during solution selection. The model warns against relying on poor substitutes for evidence such as obsolete knowledge, personal experience alone, narrow specialist habits, hype, dogma, and inappropriate benchmarking. Evidence varies in strength, and stronger evidence should carry more weight. Teams should triangulate answers using independent sources, ideally from different perspectives, to improve the reliability of conclusions.

For final comparison, the model uses multiattribute decision analysis. A decision maker identifies the alternatives and the attributes on which they should be judged. The attribute set should be complete, operational, decomposable, nonredundant, and minimal. Typical attributes include likelihood of success, timeliness, speed, and cost.

Each alternative is scored on each attribute, weights are assigned to the attributes, and a weighted score is computed. The model references SMARTER and Rank Order Centroid weighting as practical decision tools. A provisional decision is then made, followed by sensitivity analysis to test whether the ranking remains stable under different assumptions. The stage ends with a preferred solution and a clear record of why it was chosen over the alternatives.

The model also encourages capturing quick wins. Easy problems should not be ignored simply because they are small. Many individually modest actions can create meaningful cumulative value.

## Solution Promotion and Communication
Choosing a solution is not enough. The model emphasizes the need to persuade stakeholders, change thinking and behavior, and communicate the solution effectively.

Communication begins by defining the objective. Teams should decide what the audience thinks now, what they should think after the presentation, what they do now, and what they should do afterward. This from-to and think-do framing clarifies the intended effect of communication.

Storytelling is presented as a practical method for making complex findings understandable and memorable. Stories create anticipation, connect complex elements, and improve recall. They also allow emotion to be used appropriately in support of persuasion.

The model recommends building presentations around a clear storyline and distilling that storyline into slide taglines. Each slide should contain one main idea, stated as a full declarative sentence, and supported by evidence in the body of the slide. This assertion-evidence structure improves logic, recall, and discussion quality.

Persuasion should draw on ethos, pathos, logos, and timing. Credibility matters, but unsupported authority should not substitute for evidence. Emotion can help move audiences, but it must remain aligned with substance. Logic remains essential, especially when advising decision makers. The audience should receive the right level of detail, in the right sequence, without being overwhelmed by the analytical process itself.

Effective communication also depends on design. Slides should adapt to the audience, maximize signal over noise, use effective redundancy, remain clear and self-sufficient, and avoid unnecessary clutter. Visuals should be selected for explanatory power rather than decoration.

## DO: Implement Solutions
The `DO` stage translates a selected solution into action. This stage covers implementation, project management, personal effectiveness, teamwork, leadership, communication, stakeholder management, negotiation, risk management, and closure.

Implementation still benefits from divergent and convergent thinking. Teams may begin by identifying multiple possible actions and then selecting the most relevant ones. Throughout execution, the model emphasizes honesty with oneself, reliance on evidence, and caution against overconfidence, overtrust in experts, and overreliance on intuition.

The model explicitly states that problem solving is not merely about producing answers. It is also about convincing key stakeholders, leading teams effectively, managing trade-offs, and executing with discipline. Project management therefore becomes central. Teams should define goals, intermediate milestones, priorities, deliverables, deadlines, roles, resources, and dependencies.

A project charter should summarize the project's critical information. Stakeholders should understand what the project will deliver, what it will not deliver, how long it will take, how much it will cost, what benefits it should create, what drawbacks it should eliminate, and how it will be completed. Clear agreement on these points prevents scope creep and misunderstandings.

The work plan should break the project into pieces, assign deadlines, identify dependencies, allocate resources, and account for the reality that projects often take longer than expected. Success should be defined through explicit metrics and key performance indicators.

The model gives considerable attention to leadership and team design. Different team forms are appropriate under different conditions, including face-to-face teams, virtual teams, surgical teams, co-acting teams, and fluid "sand dune" teams. Team leaders often have responsibility that exceeds formal authority, so they must coordinate, influence, and maintain trust.

Effective teams combine technical skill with social skill. Heterogeneous teams may be most useful in the early stages of solving complex problems, while more homogeneous teams may help during implementation. Team effectiveness is improved by emotional intelligence, good listening, structured hiring, clear expectations, appropriate delegation, constructive feedback, and leadership styles adapted to the situation.

The model highlights emotional intelligence through four components: self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, and relationship management. These capacities support resilient leadership, productive conflict, and psychologically safe collaboration.

Meetings should be run with purpose. Objectives should be clear before the meeting, discussion should be facilitated impartially, and agreements should be summarized. The group dynamic should be managed actively so that dominant voices do not suppress quieter ones and debate remains focused on ideas rather than personalities.

Communication during implementation should be direct, precise, audience-aware, and action oriented. This includes effective writing, clear table-of-contents structures, concise emails, active listening, regular feedback, and continuous coordination. Teams should know who owns each task, what action is required, and when it is due.

Stakeholder and client management are also essential. The model stresses credibility, proactivity, expectation management, negotiation skill, and the ability to distinguish people from the problem so that interests remain central. Teams should manage risks actively by transferring, mitigating, avoiding, or accepting them as appropriate.

The stage concludes with debriefing, sharing credit, monitoring key metrics, maintaining documentation, and preserving knowledge in a central repository.

## REVIEW: Reflect and Improve
The final stage is review. Review ensures that the process itself remains effective and that teams learn from both success and failure.

The model recognizes that not every problem requires a full analysis every time. Independent modules can be applied selectively based on judgment and constraints. However, skipping framing or diagnosis entirely creates a high risk of solving the wrong problem. When time pressure is severe, the model recommends reducing effort carefully rather than bypassing essential logic.

Complex decision making is recursive by nature. Iteration between stages is normal and often necessary. If teams are not iterating at all, that may indicate a superficial understanding of the problem.

Review should include both process review and personal review. Process review examines whether the chosen analytical modules were appropriate, whether the time allocated to each stage was sufficient, whether stakeholder involvement was useful, and whether the issue maps and decision structures were insightful. Personal review focuses on tendencies such as overconfidence, confirmation bias, satisficing, weak feedback habits, and overreliance on intuition.

The model encourages a learning mentality. Teams and individuals should accept manageable failure as part of growth, distinguish errors of commission from errors of omission, and reward failures that produce valuable learning at low cost. They should maintain humility while still acting decisively, acknowledge what they know and do not know, and use feedback to calibrate judgment.

Trust should be extended carefully. Evidence should remain central, and sources should be checked for patterns of deception, bias, and motivation. Resilience should be cultivated through optimism, flexibility, strong social support, and active coping.

## Conclusion
The Complex Problem Solution Process Model offers a disciplined way to solve hard problems without collapsing into intuition, habit, or superficial analysis. It starts by framing the right problem, moves through evidence-based diagnosis, generates and compares meaningful alternatives, executes through strong leadership and project discipline, and ends with reflection and learning.

What makes the model valuable is not only its structure, but its insistence on clarity, evidence, logic, iteration, and communication. It treats problem solving as both an analytical and human process. The right answer must be found, explained, supported, implemented, and improved over time. In that sense, the model is not simply a method for solving problems. It is a method for thinking responsibly in complex environments.
